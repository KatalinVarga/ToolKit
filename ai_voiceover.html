AI Voiceover Guide

How to record your voice sample for AI voice cloning

Why This Matters

Recording your voice sample

To use your voice in AI-supported lecture production, we need one clean voice recording.
It's a one-off, roughly 8–12 minute session — the quieter and clearer it is, the better your AI voice will sound.

meeting_roomRecording Environment

  • Choose a small, quiet room with soft furnishings — a bedroom or study is ideal.
  • Avoid large, echoing rooms like kitchens.
  • Close windows and doors, and turn off fans, TVs, radios and notifications.
  • Record when background noise (traffic, children, pets) is minimal.

smartphoneDevice & Format

  • A smartphone is fine — a USB microphone and laptop is even better, if you have one.
  • Android: Hi-Q MP3 Recorder. Apple: Audio Recorder (WAV, M4A).
  • Set the format to WAV, highest quality setting — avoid MP3 if possible.
  • Hold the phone about 20–30cm from your mouth.

Do

  • Speak naturally, in your normal teaching voice
  • Sit comfortably, don't rush
  • Keep a consistent microphone distance
  • Record everything in one continuous session if possible

Don't

  • Whisper or over-enunciate artificially
  • Change your accent or speaking style
  • Apply filters, noise reduction, or audio effects
  • Record in noisy environments
If you make a mistake: pause briefly, repeat the sentence clearly, and carry on. Don't stop and restart the whole recording — we'll edit the audio as needed. Consistency matters more than perfection.
warningBefore sending: Save the file as Your Full Name AI Voice Recording (e.g. "John Doe AI Voice Recording"), and email it as a WAV attachment. If it's too large, use WeTransfer or a similar service and send us the link.
If Your Module Uses AI Voiceover

Writing your transcript for an AI voice

Some lecture videos are produced using an AI voice instead of a recorded human voiceover.
If this applies to your module, your transcript needs a few small adjustments so the AI reads it naturally, accurately, and at the right pace.

Write it this way

  • Spell out abbreviations in full on first use — e.g. "Artificial Intelligence (AI)", not just "AI"
  • Spell out numbers and symbols in words — "forty-two per cent", not "42%"
  • Keep sentences short and clear — split anything over 25 words
  • Use commas and full stops to control pacing
  • Add a blank line between major sections for a natural pause — sparingly, and consistently
  • Submit plain, unformatted text only

Avoid this

  • Phonetic spellings or pronunciation guides in brackets
  • Ellipses, em dashes, or exclamation marks
  • Brackets for side comments — write these as a separate sentence instead
  • Bullet points, bold text, Markdown, or HTML tags — these get read aloud literally
  • American spelling — the voice model is set to British English and may mispronounce or switch accent

Voice Cloning

If we're recording a clone of your voice

If we're creating a clone of your voice for AI lecture delivery, you'll be asked to record the training script below aloud.
It covers eight sections — a neutral introduction, phonetic coverage, academic delivery style, numbers and technical content, instructional tone, emphasis and contrast, conversational tone, and emotional variation —
designed to capture the full range of your natural speaking voice.

AI Voice Training Script — Academic Lecture Example

Read each section clearly, at a natural academic pace. Do not rush. Pause briefly between sentences, and maintain a consistent, confident tone throughout — as if speaking directly to a group of engaged students. Takes about 8–12 minutes in full.

Section 1 — Natural Academic Introduction (Neutral Tone)

Welcome to this module. My name is [Name], and today we will be exploring the foundational principles of research design. By the end of this session, you should be able to identify the key stages of the research cycle, distinguish between qualitative and quantitative approaches, and begin to formulate a research question relevant to your field of study.

Section 2 — Phonetic Coverage (All Core English Sounds)

The scope of this subject extends across several disciplines, including psychology, sociology, education, and public health. Each field brings its own methodological traditions, yet the underlying logic of rigorous enquiry remains consistent. Whether you are measuring attitudes, analysing narratives, or observing behaviour, the same principles of validity, reliability, and ethical responsibility apply.

Section 3 — Academic Lecture Style (Primary Target Delivery)

Let us begin with the research question. This is the single most important decision you will make in any research project. A well-formed research question is specific, measurable, and grounded in an identifiable gap in the existing literature. It should be ambitious enough to generate new knowledge, yet realistic enough to be answered within your available resources and timeframe.

Section 4 — Numbers, Dates, and Technical Content

Between nineteen ninety-five and two thousand and fifteen, the number of peer-reviewed publications in this field increased by approximately three hundred and forty per cent. In two thousand and twenty-two, over forty-two thousand studies were registered with the relevant international database. These figures reflect not only growing academic interest but also increasing methodological rigour across the discipline.

Section 5 — Questions and Instructional Tone

At this point, you may be asking yourself: how do I know if my research question is specific enough? A useful test is to ask whether a colleague in your department — someone familiar with the field but not your specific project — would be able to understand exactly what you intend to investigate. If the answer is no, the question needs to be refined.

Section 6 — Emphasis and Contrast

It is important to distinguish between a research question and a research aim. An aim describes what you intend to achieve. A question asks what you want to find out. Both are necessary, but they serve different purposes. The question drives your methodology; the aim frames your contribution.

Section 7 — Natural Conversational Academic Tone

Think of it this way. When you sit down to plan a project, you are not simply deciding what to study — you are deciding how to think about a problem. The question you choose shapes everything that follows: the literature you read, the data you collect, the analysis you conduct, and ultimately the conclusions you draw.

Section 8 — Emotional Variation (Engaged and Encouraging)

This can feel overwhelming at first, and that is entirely understandable. Research design involves a great deal of uncertainty, particularly in the early stages. But that uncertainty is not a weakness — it is precisely where good research begins. The willingness to sit with an open question, to resist the urge to jump to answers, is one of the most valuable skills you will develop as a researcher.

End of training script.
